Integrated Quantum Sensors

  1. Home
Dense ensembles of quantum spins are among the most sensitive probes of magnetic fields ever devised, but their full potential is locked behind a fundamental challenge: positional disorder in solid-state systems induces rapid dephasing that prevents coherent, collective behavior. Integrating nanoscale magnetic gradient control with engineered many-body interactions offers a route to unlock this potential, enabling disorder-robust collective dynamics that translate directly into sensing and imaging capabilities beyond the reach of classical systems.

Collective Quantum Dynamics for Enhanced Sensing

Quantum sensors based on dense spin ensembles offer exceptional magnetic field sensitivity, with applications ranging from nanoscale imaging of biological systems to probing condensed matter phases. The underlying resource is the quantum coherence of a large number of interacting spins, but harnessing this resource requires overcoming a key obstacle: in solid-state systems, spins are positionally disordered, and the resulting variation in local coupling strengths drives rapid dephasing that destroys collective behavior before it can be exploited.

Collective many-body dynamics (where an extended quantum system evolves in a coherent, correlated fashion) can in principle reduce quantum projection noise below the standard quantum limit and amplify weak signals. Such dynamics have been demonstrated in ultracold atomic platforms with engineered all-to-all interactions, but realizing them in a practical, room-temperature solid-state sensor has remained out of reach due to disorder.

In [1], we demonstrate that this barrier can be overcome by combining two ingredients: time-dependent nanoscale magnetic field gradients and Floquet-engineered SU(2)-symmetric spin interactions. The gradient imprints a spatially modulated spin texture (a conical spin spiral) across a dense ensemble of nitrogen-vacancy (NV) centers in diamond. Under a Floquet pulse sequence that engineers an SU(2)-symmetric Heisenberg interaction, nearby spins in a long-wavelength spiral are nearly co-aligned, which suppresses the divergent local energy scales responsible for disorder-induced dephasing. The spiral mode thereby becomes a protected, slow hydrodynamic mode of the interacting system.

The resulting dynamics closely resemble one-axis twisting (OAT), a canonical model for metrologically useful entanglement generation, with a precession rate that follows a cosine dependence on the spiral cone angle, and a quality factor ω/γ that clearly exceeds the theoretical limit set by local dynamics in a disordered Ising system. These observations demonstrate collective nonlinear dynamics in a positionally disordered three-dimensional dipolar spin system for the first time.

A key enabler of this work is the integration of the spin ensemble with a custom two-layer nanofabricated gradient chip. Pulsed currents through micron-scale gold coils generate magnetic field gradients up to ~2.2 mT/µm, switchable in ~15 ns, enabling both the preparation of nanoscale spin textures and Fourier magnetic imaging (FMI) of the spin polarization with sub-20 nm spatial resolution. Two device geometries are used: a bulk diamond plate and a high-density diamond nanobeam, the latter shaped to enhance gradient homogeneity and fluorescence collection.

Together, these results establish a platform for interaction-enhanced quantum metrology and nanoscale magnetic resonance imaging under ambient conditions. The approach is scalable: theoretical analysis predicts that the quality factor can grow as √N with system size due to the separation of timescales between spiral relaxation and exchange-driven twisting, pointing toward substantial, practical metrological gain with dense solid-state spin sensors.
